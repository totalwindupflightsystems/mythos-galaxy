Writing Conventions

"The viewer should say: damn, that makes sense. And even when you do the right correct thing, shit still gets fucked up."

The Seven Emotional Textures

Every scene must carry at least one of these felt-weight skills. Not themes — textures: the way a moment feels in the viewer's body.

Mortality

The weight of knowing something will end. Not sadness — the way knowing it makes every moment heavier.

Never say "they were sad about dying." Show the choice made differently because time is finite.

Grief

The specific shape of absence. The exact thing missing and its exact shape in the room.

Never say "they were grieving." Show what they reach for that isn't there anymore.

The Missed Window

The moment that was available and is now closed forever.

Never say "they missed their chance." Show them looking at the closed door, knowing which side they're on.

Trust

The active decision to make yourself vulnerable. Trust isn't a feeling — it's a bet you place with your body.

Never say "I trust you" unless it's immediately tested. Show the hesitation, then the leap.

The Irreversible

Some things cannot be undone. The word said that can't be unsaid.

Never say "it can't be undone." Show them trying to undo it anyway — and failing.

Wonder and Awe

The moment when the universe is bigger than you knew and the only response is silence.

Never say "it was beautiful." Show what they stop doing. Who stops mid-sentence.

Hope and Dread

Held simultaneously. Not hope OR dread — hoping while knowing the other outcome is just as likely.

Never say "they were hopeful but afraid." Show the balance — hope in the eyes, dread in the hands.

Primary Writing Rules

  1. Never say "they were sad." Show what sadness does — the thing not done, the sentence not finished.
  2. Never say "they were afraid." Show what fear costs — the choice not made, the person not called.
  3. Never say "they loved each other." Show what love costs — the sacrifice made, the silence kept.
  4. Every scene must advance at least one plot AND reveal something about a character.
  5. Solutions must use established elements in clever new ways. No new technology in Act 3.
  6. Failure isn't incompetence. The crew does the correct thing. It doesn't work because the situation changed.
  7. The right call still has a body count. Good choices have costs. The universe doesn't care about effort.

The "Shit Still Gets Fucked Up" Standard

The test for every episode resolution: Did the crew do the smart thing? Did it fail for a reason that makes sense? Did the failure feel like life, not like the writers being cruel? The Right Call → Right Execution → Still Costs Something → That's Not Darkness, That's Honesty.
